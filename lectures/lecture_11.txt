# Lecture 11: Numerical Differentiation

This lecture introduces numerical differentiation techniques, moving beyond analytical formulas (like the product or chain rule) to estimate derivatives for discrete data or complex functions. The core approach involves using **interpolating polynomials** to approximate a function and then taking the derivative of that polynomial to estimate the function's derivative.

---

## 1. Fundamental Theory

Numerical differentiation relies on the limit definition of a derivative:


$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$



In a numerical context, we use a small, non-zero $h$ to calculate the slope of a **secant line**, which approximates the **tangent line** at point $x$.

### The Polynomial Approach

To quantify error and improve accuracy, we represent the function $f(x)$ as:


$$f(x) = P_n(x) + R_n(x)$$


Where:

* 
$P_n(x)$ is the interpolating polynomial of degree $n$.


* 
$R_n(x)$ is the remainder (error) formula.



By differentiating both sides, we get:


$$f'(x) = P_n'(x) + R_n'(x)$$



---

## 2. Derivative Estimation Methods

### A. Two-Point Forward Difference

This method uses two points: $x_1$ and $x_2 = x_1 + h$.

* 
**Formula:** $f'(x_1) \approx \frac{f(x_1 + h) - f(x_1)}{h}$ 


* 
**Error Order:** $O(h)$ (First-order accurate).


* 
**Remainder:** $-\frac{h}{2} f''(c)$ for some $c$ in the interval.



### B. Three-Point Symmetric (Central) Difference

This method uses three points: $x_1 - h$, $x_1$, and $x_1 + h$. It leverages **symmetry** for higher accuracy without requiring extra evaluation points in the final formula.

* 
**Formula:** $f'(x_1) \approx \frac{f(x_1 + h) - f(x_1 - h)}{2h}$ 


* 
**Error Order:** $O(h^2)$ (Second-order accurate).


* 
**Remainder:** $-\frac{h^2}{6} f'''(c)$.



### C. Three-Point One-Sided Difference

Used when data is only available in one direction (e.g., at the start of a data set where $x < 0$ is unavailable). It uses $x_1, x_1+h,$ and $x_1+2h$.

* 
**Formula:** $f'(x_1) \approx \frac{f(x_1 + 2h) - f(x_1)}{2h}$ 


* 
**Error Order:** $O(h^2)$ (Second-order accurate).


* 
**Note:** While the order is $O(h^2)$, the error constant ($\frac{h^2}{3}$) is **twice as large** as the symmetric version.



---

## 3. Comparison of Estimators

| Method | Points Used | Formula | Error Term | Accuracy |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Forward Difference** | $x_1, x_1+h$ | $\frac{f(x_1+h)-f(x_1)}{h}$ | $\frac{h}{2} | f''(c) |
| **Symmetric Difference** | $x_1-h, x_1+h$ | $\frac{f(x_1+h)-f(x_1-h)}{2h}$ | $\frac{h^2}{6} | f'''(c) |
| **One-Sided (3-pt)** | $x_1, x_1+2h$ | $\frac{f(x_1+2h)-f(x_1)}{2h}$ | $\frac{h^2}{3} | f'''(c) |

---

## 4. Key Takeaways

* 
**Symmetry is Superior:** Symmetric points provide the best accuracy for the absolute error because the error constant is minimized.


* 
**Assumptions on $f$:** To increase the order of accuracy (e.g., from $O(h)$ to $O(h^2)$), you must assume the function is more "smooth" (e.g., $f'''$ must exist rather than just $f''$).


* 
**Application Constraints:** One-sided formulas are necessary if you lack data points on one side of the target value, despite their slightly lower accuracy compared to symmetric formulas.
