# Lecture Notes: Big Oh Reprise (Lecture 9)

These notes cover the derivation of **Big O estimates** for various polynomial approximation schemes, focusing on how convergence rates depend on the degree of the polynomial ($n$) or the spacing of sample points ($h$).

---

## Core Objective

The goal is to determine the convergence rate by bounding the **uniform distance** (maximum pointwise difference) between a function $f(x)$ and its approximation $g(x)$ over an interval $[a, b]$:


$$\max_{x \in [a, b]} |f(x) - g(x)| = O(\text{rate})$$

This allows us to control the error by increasing the polynomial degree or decreasing the sampling interval.

---

## 1. Taylor Series Convergence

Taylor series approximations rely on the **Lagrange form of the remainder** to estimate error.

### The Remainder Term

For a Taylor polynomial of degree $N$ centered at $a$, the error is:


$$|f(x) - P_N(x)| = \left| \frac{f^{(N+1)}(c)}{(N+1)!} (x-a)^{N+1} \right|$$

where $c$ is some value between $x$ and $a$.

### Convergence Rate

* **Assumptions:** We assume the derivatives of $f$ are bounded, such as $|f^{(N+1)}(c)| [cite_start]\leq C \cdot M^N$. This applies to functions like $e^{kx}$, $\sin(kx)$, and $\cos(kx)$.


* 
**Result:** By substituting the bounds into the remainder formula, we find that the error is governed by an exponential divided by a factorial:



$$\max_{x \in [a, b]} |f(x) - P_N(x)| = O\left( \frac{\tilde{M}^{N+1}}{(N+1)!} \right)$$


* 
**Conclusion:** Since factorials grow faster than exponentials, this error always goes to zero as $N \to \infty$.



---

## 2. Interpolating Polynomials

Interpolation involves finding a polynomial of degree $n$ that passes through $n+1$ specific points.

### The Remainder Term

The error for an interpolating polynomial $P_n(x)$ is:


$$|f(x) - P_n(x)| = \left| \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!} \prod_{i=1}^{n+1} (x - x_i) \right|$$

### Bounding the Product Term

To find a Big O estimate for equally spaced points (where spacing is $h$), we must bound the product $\prod |x - x_i|$:

1. 
**Logarithmic Transformation:** We take the logarithm of the product to turn it into a sum: $\sum \log|x - x_i|$.


2. 
**Integral Approximation:** For large $n$, this sum is approximated by an integral.


3. 
**Exponential Bound:** Taking the exponential of the resulting integral bound yields an estimate growing like $e^n$.



### Convergence Rate

Substituting this back into the remainder formula:


$$\max |f(x) - P_n(x)| = O\left( \frac{e^{n \cdot \text{constant}}}{(n+1)!} \right)$$

Similar to Taylor series, the factorial in the denominator ensures the error converges to zero.

---

## 3. Bernstein Polynomials

Bernstein polynomials provide a way to approximate continuous functions on the interval $[0, 1]$.

### The Formula

The $n$-th Bernstein polynomial for $f$ is:


$$B_n(f; x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} f\left(\frac{k}{n}\right) x^k (1-x)^{n-k}$$

### Deriving the Rate

To find the Big O estimate, we use a Taylor expansion of $f$ of order one and assume $f$ is **twice continuously differentiable** ($f \in C^2$).

* **Key Identities:**
* 
$B_n(1; x) = 1$ 


* 
$B_n(x; x) = x$ 


* The term involving the second derivative simplifies to $\frac{x(1-x)}{n}$.




* 
**Result:** The error is bounded by a constant times $\frac{1}{n}$:



$$\max_{x \in [0, 1]} |B_n(f; x) - f(x)| = O\left( \frac{1}{n} \right)$$



> 
> **Note:** If the function is only continuous (not $C^2$), the best convergence rate is significantly slower at $O(1/\sqrt{n})$.
>

## Summary of Convergence Rates

| Method | Convergence Rate (Big O) | Primary Requirement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Taylor Series** | $O\left( \frac{M^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \right)$ | High-order smoothness 

 |
| **Interpolation** | $O\left( \frac{e^{cn}}{(n+1)!} \right)$ | Equally spaced points 

 |
| **Bernstein** | $O\left( \frac{1}{n} \right)$ | Twice continuously differentiable ($C^2$) 

 |
