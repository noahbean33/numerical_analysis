## **Overview & Motivation**

While previous lectures focused on **polynomial approximation** (Taylor series, Lagrange, Newton, and Vandermonde methods), this lecture introduces **kernel functions** as a modern alternative for function approximation.

### **Why Kernels?**

* 
**Higher Dimensions:** Unlike splines, which are difficult to translate to higher dimensions, the algorithm for kernel methods remains unchanged whether you are working in 1D or 20D.


* 
**Modern Applications:** Kernels are a pivotal tool in **Machine Learning** (specifically Support Vector Machines) and Data Science.


* 
**Versatility:** They are used to solve initial value problems, partial differential equations (PDEs), and operator theory.



---

## **Formal Definition of a Kernel**

Let $K: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function of two variables on a set $X$. $K$ is considered a **kernel function** if it satisfies either of the following equivalent conditions:

### **1. Positive Definiteness (The Gram Matrix)**

Given any collection of distinct points $\{x_1, \dots, x_m\} \in X$, the resulting **Gram matrix** $G$ must be positive definite:


$$G = \begin{bmatrix} K(x_1, x_1) & \cdots & K(x_1, x_m) \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ K(x_m, x_1) & \cdots & K(x_m, x_m) \end{bmatrix}$$

A positive definite matrix implies that for any non-zero vector $v$, $v^T G v > 0$, ensuring the matrix is **invertible**.

### **2. Feature Maps**

A kernel can be represented as an inner product of **feature maps**:


$$K(x, y) = \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} \psi_m(x) \psi_m(y)$$

where $\{\psi_m\}$ is a collection of functions such that $\sum |\psi_m(x)|^2 < \infty$.

---

## **Key Kernel Examples**

### **Exponential Dot Product Kernel**

Also known as the **Fock space kernel**, it is defined as:


$$K(x, y) = e^{x y}$$

This is proven to be a kernel by expanding it into its Taylor series, which reveals the underlying feature functions $\psi_n(x) = \frac{x^n}{\sqrt{n!}}$.

### **Gaussian Radial Basis Function (RBF) Kernel**

This is one of the most common kernels used in applied mathematics:


$$K(x, y) = e^{-\frac{\|x - y\|^2}{\mu}}$$

* 
**Radial Nature:** The function is "centered" at a point $y$ and decays as $x$ moves further away.


* 
**Parameter $\mu$:** A scaling parameter used to adjust the kernel based on the specific data.



---

## **The Interpolation Goal**

Given a function $f$ and a collection of sample points (support points) $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^M$, we seek an interpolant $s_m(x)$ of the form:


$$s_m(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{M} w_i K(x, x_i)$$

The goal is to find the weights $w_i$ such that the interpolant matches the data exactly: $s_m(x_i) = y_i$.

---

## **The Matrix Formulation**

To find the weights, we set up a system of linear equations:


$$\begin{bmatrix} K(x_1, x_1) & \cdots & K(x_1, x_m) \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ K(x_m, x_1) & \cdots & K(x_m, x_m) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} w_1 \\ \vdots \\ w_m \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_m \end{bmatrix}$$

This can be written compactly as:


$$G \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{y}$$

Because the Gram matrix $G$ is positive definite (provided the points $x_i$ are distinct), it is guaranteed to be **invertible**. Therefore, the weight vector can be calculated as:


$$\mathbf{w} = G^{-1} \mathbf{y}$$
