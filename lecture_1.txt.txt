## I. Course Overview & Philosophy

Numerical analysis is the study of algorithms that use numerical approximation for the problems of mathematical analysis.

* 
**Analytic vs. Numerical:** In calculus, solutions often have a **closed form** (e.g., using the chain rule or integration by parts). Numerical analysis provides a framework when these analytic methods fail or when functions are only known through discrete samples.


* 
**Core Goals:** * Developing algorithms for derivatives, integrals, and root-finding.


* Demonstrating **convergence** (ensuring the algorithm actually reaches the correct value).


* Analyzing **convergence rates** to balance speed and computational cost.




* 
**Course Structure:** The class consists of four units, each culminating in a project, and two non-cumulative exams.

## II. Historical Method: Archimedes and the Value of $\pi$

Archimedes (c. 350 BC) developed the first routine for approximating $\pi$ by bounding a circle with polygons.

### 1. Initial Bounds

By using a circle of radius 1, the area is exactly $\pi$.

* 
**Upper Bound:** A circumscribed square has a side length of 2, resulting in an area of **4**.


* 
**Lower Bound:** An inscribed square creates 45-45-90 triangles with side lengths of $\sqrt{2}$, resulting in an area of **2**.


* 
**Initial Estimate:** $\pi$ is roughly 3.



### 2. General Formula for Polygons

As the number of sides doubles, the approximation improves. For the $n$-th iteration (where $n$ relates to doubling the triangles), the area of the circumscribed polygon $A_n$ is derived as:


$$A_n = 2^n \tan\left(\frac{\pi}{2^n}\right)$$

### 3. Proof of Convergence

Using the Taylor series for $\tan(x) = x + \frac{x^3}{3} + \dots$:


$$2^n \tan\left(\frac{\pi}{2^n}\right) = 2^n \left( \frac{\pi}{2^n} + \frac{(\frac{\pi}{2^n})^3}{3} + \dots \right)$$

$$= \pi + \frac{\pi^3}{3(2^{2n})} + \dots$$

As $n \to \infty$, the higher-order terms go to 0, and the area converges to $\pi$.

## III. Heron’s Method for Square Roots

Heron of Alexandria (1st Century BC) developed an iterative method to find the square root of a number $S$.

### 1. The Iterative Formula

The method is equivalent to the modern **Newton's Method** for the polynomial $x^2 - S = 0$.


$$x_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{S}{x_n} + x_n \right)$$

### 2. Example: Finding $\sqrt{2}$

* 
**Initial Guess ($x_0$):** Let $x_0 = 2$.


* 
**Iteration 1 ($x_1$):** $\frac{1}{2} (2/2 + 2) = 1.5$.


* 
**Iteration 2 ($x_2$):** $\frac{1}{2} (2/1.5 + 1.5) \approx 1.4166...$.
This reaches three significant digits of accuracy ($\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$) in only two steps.

## IV. Logarithm Tables and Arithmetic Efficiency

Before calculators, mathematicians like Henry Briggs (16th century) created tables to simplify multiplication into addition.

### 1. The Power of Logs

Since $\log(ab) = \log(a) + \log(b)$, products can be found via:

1. Lookup $\log(a)$ and $\log(b)$ in a table.


2. Add the values.


3. Perform a "reverse lookup" to find the product.



### 2. Table Construction (Briggs' Method)

Briggs looked for powers of 2 close to powers of 10.

* 
$2^{10} = 1024 \approx 10^3$.


* Therefore, $10 \log_{10}(2) \approx 3$, meaning $\log_{10}(2) \approx 0.3$.
Briggs refined these tables to 52 decimal places using successive square roots (Heron's method) and interpolation.

## V. Fermat’s Method of Quadrature

Pierre de Fermat (1607–1665) developed a way to integrate power functions ($x^n$) before the formal invention of calculus.

### 1. Geometric Partitioning

Instead of rectangles of equal width, Fermat used widths that shrink geometrically according to a ratio $r$ (where $0 < r < 1$). The interval $[0, a]$ is partitioned into points $a, ar, ar^2, ar^3, \dots$.

### 2. Summation and Result

By summing the areas of these infinite rectangles using the **geometric series formula**, Fermat determined that the area under $x^n$ from $0$ to $a$ is:


$$\text{Area} = a^{n+1} \frac{1-r}{1-r^{n+1}}$$

By factoring the denominator as $(1-r)(1 + r + r^2 + \dots + r^n)$, the $(1-r)$ terms cancel. As $r \to 1$, the denominator becomes $n+1$, yielding the modern power rule:


$$\int_0^a x^n dx = \frac{a^{n+1}}{n+1}$$
